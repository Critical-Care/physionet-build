PhysioNet

The research resource for complex physiological data.

PhysioNet is a distribution and publishing platform for physiologic data and software. It hosts large volumes of open-access and protected resources, and facilitates cooperative analysis of data, evaluation of algorithms, and the free open dissemination of information.

In cooperation with the annual Computing in Cardiology conference, PhysioNet also hosts an annual series of challenges, in which researchers address unsolved problems of clinical or basic scientific interest using data and software provided by PhysioNet.

Researchers are increasingly seeking to deposit and share data through PhysioNet. Demand has been driven by the rising recognition of the importance of open data, coupled with journals such as Nature Scientific Data including PhysioNet as a recommended repository for health data.

By providing data curation and custom access requirements, which are often required when dealing with sensitive health data, PhysioNet provides a great platform for publishing content. By sharing common data sets, and software in source form, the research community benefits from access to materials that have been rigorously scrutinized by many investigators. We invite researchers to contribute data and software. Please review our author guidelines before submitting material.

Funding

PhysioNet was originally established under the auspices of the NIH's National Center for Research Resources, which was abolished in 2011. PhysioNet has been funded since September 2007 under a cooperative agreement with the NIH's National Institute of Biomedical Imaging and Bioengineering, and the National Institute of General Medical Sciences, under grant number: 2R01GM104987-09.

With the rapid explosion of data volume, our operating costs have grown immensely. If you would like to support our mission, please consider making a donation to Physionet, or contributing to one of our open source projects.

A Brief History

Foundation, growth and impact.

  • 1980

    Landmark Database

    In the mid-1970s, members of the PhysioNet team who were then working on some of the first microcomputer-based instruments for cardiac arrhythmia monitoring, foresaw the usefulness of establishing shared databases of well-characterized ECG recordings, as a basis for evaluation, and objective comparison of algorithms for automated arrhythmia analysis. A five-year effort culminated in the publication of the MIT-BIH Arrhythmia Database in 1980, which soon became the standard reference collection of its type.

  • 1999

    PhysioNet is Released

    PhysioNet was established in 1999 as the outreach component of the Research Resource for Complex Physiologic Signals, a cooperative project initiated by a diverse group of scientists and clinicians at Boston's Beth Israel Deaconess Medical Center, Boston University, and McGill University, and the MIT group.


    The MIT group contributed its 11 databases, and the software it had developed for exploring and analyzing them, to establish PhysioBank and PhysioToolkit. Free availability of these resources catalyzed an even greater explosion of interest in them, as researchers worldwide who had no previous access to such data or software began new programs of research. These initial contributions were quickly supplemented by additional collections of data and software from their collaborators, and soon after, from many researchers worldwide.

  • 2000

    Computing in Cardiology

    Members of the PhysioNet team at MIT were preparing to host Computing in Cardiology 2000, and created the first PhysioNet/CinC Challenge. They hoped to introduce PhysioNet to international colleagues, by encouraging participation in an activity that made effective use of PhysioNet's resources to stimulate rapid progress on an unsolved problem of practical clinical significance. More than a dozen teams participated in the challenge of detecting sleep apnea from the ECG, and an annual tradition was born.

  • 2018

    New Web Platform

    New members of the LCP build a modern dynamic PhysioNet platform using Django. Work is continually ongoing to improve the site's capabilities.

  • Looking Forward

    Open Science

    PhysioNet today hosts over 90 public databases, a variety of software tools, and the annual challenge. An immense number of of global visitors leverage its resources, producing more scientific and industrial development than the individual data/software contributors ever could alone.


    The future of science relies on new findings to grow upon past research. Unfortunately, the literature has been polluted with flawed studies, resulting in the reproducibility crisis, which is especially acute in medical research and psychology.


    Give legitimacy to your studies, let the world leverage your resources, and help us work towards a better scientific future, by publishing your code and data on PhysioNet.
